FEA · Planar Truss &
Belleville Spring Washer

Two-part FEA study. Part 1 solves an 8-DOF planar truss via LU decomposition in MATLAB and verifies against LINK180 and BEAM188 elements in ANSYS. Part 2 analyses a Belleville spring washer analytically and across 2D axisymmetric and 3D solid FEA models, with large deflection enabled throughout.

Part 1 · Planar Truss
FIG. 01
Planar Truss · Geometry and Boundary Conditions
4-node planar truss with fixed supports at A and B, 20 kN downward load at node 4. Member lengths L and √2·L. 8 DOF system solved via direct stiffness method in MATLAB using LU decomposition of the global stiffness matrix.
FIG. 02
LINK180 Truss · Total Deformation
ANSYS LINK180 bar element model. Maximum deformation 27.398 mm at node 4. All 8 nodal displacements reproduced with a maximum error of 0.006% against MATLAB solution.
FIG. 03
BEAM188 Truss · Total Deformation
ANSYS BEAM188 element model including bending stiffness. Maximum deformation 27.402 mm — marginally higher than LINK180 due to bending contributions. Maximum error 0.115% against MATLAB.
FIG. 04
Nodal Displacement Comparison · MATLAB vs FEA Truss vs FEA Beam
All 8 DOF compared across three methods. MATLAB (direct stiffness), LINK180, and BEAM188 results are in close agreement throughout, confirming the validity of the analytical formulation. Maximum deviation 0.115% for BEAM188 at x₇.
Part 2 · Belleville Spring Washer
FIG. 05
2D Axisymmetric Geometry and Boundary Conditions
Cross-sectional profile showing Belleville spring dimensions. Edge A: X = free, Y = −3.72 mm (applied displacement). Vertex B: X = free, Y = 0 mm. Outer radius 100 mm, inner radius 41 mm, thickness 6.70 mm.
FIG. 06
3D Solid Geometry and Boundary Conditions
Full 3D solid model. A (inner edge): Y = −3.72 mm displacement. B (outer top edge): X = 0, Z = 0. C (outer bottom edge): Y = 0. Lateral constraints at B suppress natural radial expansion — the root cause of the 3D stiffness overestimate.
FIG. 07
2D Axisymmetric Mesh · Belleville Spring Cross-Section
Structured quad mesh of the 2D axisymmetric cross-section. Refined towards edges A and B to capture stress concentrations accurately. Grid independence achieved at ~200 elements.
FIG. 08
3D Solid Mesh · Full and Sectioned Views
High-density hex-dominant mesh on the full 3D solid (left) and a 45° sector cut-away revealing internal element structure (right). Grid independence achieved at ~50,000 elements. Circumferential mesh density drives the 3D model's computational cost relative to the 2D equivalent.
FIG. 09
Grid Independence Study · 2D and 3D Models
Directional deformation vs element count for both models. 2D (red) converges at ~200 elements to −1.7995 mm. 3D (blue) converges at ~50,000 elements to −1.771 mm. Both plateaus confirm mesh-independent solutions before final analysis runs.
FIG. 10
2D Axisymmetric · Directional Deformation (Y-Axis)
Applied displacement δ = 3.72 mm. Deformation gradient from −3.72 mm (inner edge, blue) to +0.033 mm (outer edge, red), consistent with the spring's conical geometry flattening under compression.
FIG. 11
3D Solid · Directional Deformation (Y-Axis)
Same applied displacement δ = 3.72 mm. Rotationally symmetric deformation field — inner bore (blue, −3.72 mm) to outer rim (red, +0.020 mm). Lateral constraints at the outer edge introduce artificial circumferential stiffness not present in the 2D model.
FIG. 12
2D Axisymmetric · von Mises Stress
Peak stress 2597.9 MPa concentrated at the inner edge (A), the expected location for a Belleville spring under compression. The stress gradient decays rapidly toward the outer radius, consistent with the Norton force-deflection model.
FIG. 13
3D Solid · von Mises Stress
Peak stress 2419.3 MPa at the inner bore edge, showing the same radial decay pattern as the 2D model. The 3D model captures the full circumferential stress state and shows the stress ring clearly around the inner edge.
FIG. 14
Reaction Force and Spring Constant · Method Comparison
Left: reaction forces at δ = 3.72 mm — manufacturer (68,204 N), analytical (68,190 N, 0.86% error), 2D FEA (66,563 N, 2.42% error), 3D FEA (78,760 N, 15.13% error). Right: spring constant k — analytical 12,956 N/mm, 2D 12,291 N/mm, 3D 20,580 N/mm (+58.7%). The 3D overestimate is attributed to over-constrained lateral boundary conditions at the outer edge suppressing radial expansion.
